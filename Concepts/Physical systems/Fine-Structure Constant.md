Fundamental dimensionless constant characterizing electromagnetic interactions.
$$\alpha = \frac{e^2}{\hbar c} \approx \frac{1}{137.036}$$
In SI units: $\alpha = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar c}$
## Properties
**Dimensionless**: Has the same value in any unit system
**Small parameter**: $\alpha \approx 0.0073 \ll 1$
**Fundamental**: Appears throughout quantum electrodynamics (QED)
## Physical Meaning
$\alpha$ characterizes the strength of electromagnetic interactions:
- Ratio of electron's electrostatic potential energy to its rest mass energy at distance $r \sim \hbar/m_e c$ (Compton wavelength)
- Measures coupling strength in quantum electrodynamics
- Determines energy scale of atomic physics relative to particle rest masses
## Why "Fine-Structure" Constant?
Named because it determines the size of **fine-structure splitting** in atomic spectra - small corrections to energy levels from relativistic effects.
Fine-structure splitting $\sim \alpha^2 \times$ (main energy level) $\sim 10^{-4}$ eV
## Role in Atomic Physics
**Energy scale**: 
$$E_{\text{atomic}} \sim m_e c^2 \alpha^2 \sim (0.5 \text{ MeV}) \times (1/137)^2 \sim 13.6 \text{ eV}$$

## Expansion Parameter
In QED, calculations use $\alpha$ as expansion parameter:
- Leading order: $\alpha$
- Next order: $\alpha^2$
- Fine-structure: $\alpha^2$ corrections
- Hyperfine: $\alpha^3$ or higher
Since $\alpha \ll 1$, perturbation series converges rapidly.
